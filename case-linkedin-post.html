The Post That
Never Appeared

A quiet UX failure inside LinkedIn's content publishing flow — where a successful submission triggers silence instead of confirmation, and users are left wondering if anything happened at all.

Product
LinkedIn
Type
Feedback Design Audit
Focus
Post-action feedback
Published
Medium · Design Bootcamp
01 — Problem

You posted. But
did anything happen?

You write a LinkedIn post. You tap the "Post" button. The screen transitions back to your feed. Your post is not visible. There's no confirmation toast. No success message. No indication that the post is being processed. Just the feed — unchanged.

"The absence of feedback is itself a form of feedback — and it's always bad feedback."

This is a classic feedback gap — a moment where the user has completed an action but the product provides no acknowledgement. The result is anxiety, repeated tapping, and broken trust in whether the product actually works.

02 — Research

Why feedback gaps
are uniquely damaging

I mapped the emotional journey of a LinkedIn content creator through the post-submission moment and identified four dimensions of the problem.

🤔
Uncertainty creates anxiety
When a high-effort action (writing a post) receives no confirmation, users immediately question whether the action worked. Did it post? Did it fail silently? Should they try again?
🔁
Leads to duplicate posts
Without confirmation, many users tap "Post" again — resulting in duplicate posts. LinkedIn's post-visibility delay (the feed doesn't show the post immediately due to algorithmic processing) amplifies this.
💔
Trust damage on a platform built on trust
LinkedIn's professional context makes this worse. A user who posts career updates, professional opinions, or company news needs to know their content went through. The stakes are higher than social media.
🔧
Easy fix, significant impact
A simple post-submission toast notification with "Your post is live — view it here" would eliminate virtually all the anxiety this gap creates. The technical lift is minimal; the UX impact is large.
03 — Design Process

Closing the
feedback gap

01
Map all post-submission states
Documented every possible outcome after tapping "Post" — success, queued for processing, failed, draft saved — and what feedback the current UI provides in each case. Only one state (failure) received any feedback. All others were silent.
02
Design a contextual confirmation pattern
Designed a toast notification that fires immediately on submission — regardless of whether the post is visible in the feed yet. "Your post is being processed" is better than silence. "Your post is live" with a direct link is better still.
03
Add post-visibility anchoring
When the user's feed doesn't immediately show their new post, a persistent indicator ("Your recent post — tap to view") prevents the assumption that something failed. The feed sorts chronologically and algorithmically — users don't know why their post isn't at the top.
04
Write feedback-first UX copy
Replaced the implicit silence with explicit confirmation. Every confirmation message includes the next best action — view the post, see engagement, or edit. The UX writing does as much work as the design pattern.
04 — Outcomes

The principle behind
the fix

1
Toast notification
eliminates all doubt
0
Screens that should
ever be silent post-action
∞
Users who deserve
to know their action worked

The lesson is simple and universal: every user action deserves an acknowledgement. Whether it's posting content, saving a form, or completing a purchase — silence is never neutral. It's always read as ambiguity, and ambiguity is always a UX failure.
