Portfolio/Case Studies/Rapido Dead-End Screens
Mobile UX · Error States · High-Stakes Friction

Tapping for
Nothing

Rapido — India's fastest-growing cab and bike taxi app — has a recurring UX problem: dead-end screens. States where the user is stranded with no clear path forward, no explanation of what went wrong, and no immediate way to recover. A teardown of high-stakes friction.

UX Researcher & Writer
Type
Error State Audit
Tools

01 — Observation

Stranded in the app
while stranded in real life

You're standing at a street corner. You need a ride. You open Rapido, search for a captain, watch the loader spin, and then land on a screen that says something went wrong. There's no retry button. No explanation. No guidance. Just a screen that assumes you know what to do next — when you don't.

This is a dead-end screen: a UI state that terminates the user's flow with no escape route, no context, and no next step. In a cab-booking app — where the user is often outside, time-pressured, and relying on the product for physical transportation — this isn't just bad UX. It's a genuine failure of the product's core promise.

A dead-end screen in a rideshare app isn't a UX problem. It's leaving your user stranded on a street corner.

02 — Analysis

Why dead-end screens
hurt more in high-stakes apps

🚦
Context is everything
In a map or productivity app, a dead-end is annoying. In a rideshare app where physical mobility depends on the product working, it's urgent. The stakes change the severity of the UX failure.
📵
No alternative in the moment
When the app fails mid-booking, users can't easily switch to a competitor instantly. They're in the flow — and the failure cascades into real-world consequences.
❌
No error clarity
Rapido's error screens frequently omit the reason for failure. 'Something went wrong' with no detail forces the user to guess: is it their network? The captain? The payment method?
🔄
No retry path
The most critical missing element: a clear 'Try Again' CTA with the user's booking state preserved. Most error screens drop the user back to the home screen, losing all their input.
03 — The Fix

Designing error states
as first-class experiences

The principle is simple: error states are user experiences, not edge cases. They should be designed with the same care as happy-path flows — perhaps more, because the user's emotional state at an error moment is already stressed.

Every Rapido error screen needs three elements: a specific, plain-language explanation of what went wrong; a single, clear action to recover; and preservation of the user's booking state so they don't have to start over. Add a contextual fallback — 'No bikes available? Try auto' — and the dead-end becomes a pivot point instead of a wall.

04 — Takeaway

Error design
is product design

The apps that earn the most trust are not the ones that fail the least — every app fails sometimes. They're the ones that handle failure with the same care they put into their best features. A product that strands you when something goes wrong quickly teaches you not to depend on it.

Rapido's core product is excellent. Its error states betray that excellence every day. The gap between the happy path and the failure path is where trust is built or lost.
