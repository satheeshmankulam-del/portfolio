Designing for
"I Don't Care" Users

What happens when your user has zero interest in engaging? A study of low-intent behaviour patterns, passive users, and how design can create just enough pull — without tipping into manipulation.

Type
Behavioural UX Research
Focus
Engagement · Intent
Tools
Figma · Miro · Writing
Published
Medium · Design Bootcamp
01 — Problem

Most UX assumes
users want to be there

Product design almost always assumes a motivated user — someone who arrived with intent, knows what they want, and is ready to engage. But a significant portion of real users are passive: they opened the app out of habit, scrolled without purpose, or arrived via a notification they barely registered.

"Designing for 'I don't care' users isn't about tricking them. It's about creating just enough friction to pause the scroll — and enough value to make them glad they did."

The challenge: how do you design for someone whose default state is disengagement — without resorting to dark patterns, notification spam, or manufactured urgency?

02 — Research

Understanding
low-intent behaviour

I studied three distinct passive user archetypes and the specific interaction patterns that cause them to engage — or disengage further.

🌀
The Scroller — habits without goals
Opens the app because they always open the app. No specific intent. Will leave within 30 seconds unless something creates a pattern interrupt — a visual anomaly, a number that means something personal, an unexpected moment of relevance.
👀
The Browser — ambient awareness
Has a vague interest in the category but no specific need today. Will engage with content that feels like ambient value — easy to consume, low commitment, personally relevant. The design task: reduce commitment friction to zero.
🔄
The Returner — forgotten intent
Was engaged once, lost the habit. Returns occasionally but needs a re-onboarding moment — a "here's where you left off" or "here's what's new" that reconnects them to the value they once found.
⚖️
The manipulation line
Low-intent design sits dangerously close to dark patterns. The difference: dark patterns extract engagement through anxiety and FOMO. Good design creates engagement through genuine value and reduced friction.
03 — Design Principles

Nudge without
manipulation

01
Personalised ambient value
For scrollers and browsers, the first 3 seconds must feel personally relevant. This isn't about trapping them — it's about showing them something that makes them feel the product knows them. Personalisation at the content level, not notification level.
02
Zero-commitment entry points
Every piece of content or feature that targets low-intent users should be completable in one tap with no commitment. No account creation, no "continue where you left off" pressure, no progress bars that imply obligation.
03
Pattern interrupts without anxiety
Visual or interactive interrupts that break the scroll — unusual layout, a number that means something real (your streak, your savings, your usage) — work for low-intent users without creating FOMO or urgency pressure.
04
Re-onboarding for returners
Users who return after a gap need a single-screen "here's where you left off" moment. Not a full tutorial — a warm contextual hand-off that re-establishes the product's value in the context of their personal history with it.
