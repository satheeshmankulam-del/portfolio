When AI Knows
Me Too Well

The fine line between helpful personalisation and creepy familiarity — and how UX design either builds or destroys user trust in AI-powered products.

Type
AI Design Research
Focus
Privacy · Trust · AI UX
Tools
Figma · Research · Writing
Published
Medium · Design Bootcamp
01 — Problem

Helpful or
surveillance?

AI-powered personalisation can make a product feel like it truly understands you. But there's a tipping point — a moment where "this knows me" becomes "this is watching me" — and once crossed, trust is very hard to recover.

"The problem isn't that AI knows too much. It's that it shows too much of what it knows, at the wrong moment."

The design challenge for AI-powered products: how do you use user data to create genuine value without making the user feel surveilled, profiled, or violated?

02 — Research

The four dimensions
of AI trust

👁️
Visibility of inference
Users become uncomfortable when AI reveals the depth of its inference — "Because you visited three mortgage calculators this week..." is helpful information delivered in a way that feels like surveillance. The insight is valid; the framing is wrong.
⏰
Timing of personalisation
Personalisation shown at the right moment feels helpful. The same personalisation shown out of context feels intrusive. A recommendation immediately after a relevant action is natural. The same recommendation on first launch feels premature.
🎛️
User control and transparency
The difference between trust and distrust is often just a visible control. "We noticed X — based on this" with an option to dismiss or adjust transforms a potentially creepy inference into a transparent, respectable feature.
🔒
Data source clarity
Users are more comfortable with personalisation when they understand its source. "Based on your recent searches" is acceptable. "Based on your location history combined with purchase patterns" is alarming — even if it's exactly what the first one means.
03 — Design Principles

Building trust in
AI-powered products

01
Show the value, not the inference
Don't expose the data model. "You might like this" is fine. "Because you read 3 articles about minimalism last Tuesday" is unsettling. The value (the recommendation) is what the user wants — the inference pathway should be invisible unless the user asks.
02
Always provide a control
Every AI-generated recommendation, suggestion, or personalised element should have a visible "not for me" or "why am I seeing this" option. This single design pattern reduces the feeling of surveillance dramatically.
03
Contextual timing over constant presence
AI personalisation should fire at moments of clear relevance — not on every screen. Showing personalisation at 100% frequency makes it invisible and also normalises data usage in a way that can feel oppressive.
04
Privacy-first language in AI copy
UX writing for AI features should minimise the perception of data collection even when data is being used. "Suggestions for you" feels benign. "Inferred from your profile" does not. Same data, completely different trust response.
