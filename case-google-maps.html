Smarter Navigation
with Vehicle Profiles

A UX case study on Google Maps & Android Auto — exploring how a single missing feature creates friction for millions of drivers every day, and what the solution could look like.

Role
UX Researcher & Designer
Type
Feature UX Case Study
Tools
Figma · Miro · Medium
Status
Published · 2025
01 — Problem

Google Maps doesn't know
what you're driving.

Every time you open Google Maps for navigation, it treats you the same way whether you're on a bicycle, in a compact car, or driving a large van. There's no awareness of vehicle type — and that single gap creates friction that affects millions of people every day.

When you switch from your phone to Android Auto, the interface changes — but the logic doesn't. You still get the same routing, the same POI suggestions, the same information density. The context of being in a vehicle — hands-free, larger display, focused on driving — isn't reflected in how the product behaves.

"A truck driver gets routed under a low bridge. A van driver is shown parking spots too small for their vehicle. A camper gets highway routes instead of scenic drives."

These aren't edge cases. They're daily friction points for a significant portion of Google Maps' 2 billion users.

02 — Research

Understanding the gap

I mapped the journey of a typical driver who uses both Google Maps on their phone and Android Auto in their car. The context switch between the two reveals a fundamental assumption baked into the product: the user's vehicle doesn't matter.

🚗
Vehicle type shapes routing needs
Height restrictions, weight limits, narrow roads, parking dimensions — all vary dramatically by vehicle type, and Maps ignores all of them by default.
📱→🚙
Context changes on Android Auto
Hands-free mode, larger display, driver focus state — the interface acknowledges these, but the underlying navigation intelligence doesn't adapt.
🔁
Same car, every day — zero memory
Most users drive the same vehicle daily. Google Maps saves routes, favourites, home — but not the thing the user interacts with most: their car.
📍
POI suggestions are vehicle-blind
Parking suggestions, fuel stops, EV charging — all shown without vehicle context. A petrol car gets EV charger suggestions. A small car gets multi-storey parking for trucks.
03 — Design Process

Designing the solution

01
Define the Vehicle Profile
A one-time setup screen during Google Maps onboarding or in Settings — vehicle type (car, van, truck, EV, motorbike), dimensions (optional), fuel type. Syncs across phone and Android Auto via Google account.
02
Adapt Route Options
When a vehicle profile exists, the routing engine applies relevant filters by default — no low bridges for tall vehicles, no narrow roads for wide ones. Users can still override manually.
03
Context-Aware Android Auto UI
On Android Auto, the right-side info panel surfaces vehicle-relevant data: height clearance warnings, EV charge status, fuel-efficient routes — based on the saved profile, not generic defaults.
04
Smart POI Suggestions
Parking, fuel, and stop suggestions filtered by vehicle type. A van gets larger parking options. An EV gets charging stations ordered by compatibility and wait time, not just proximity.
04 — Outcomes

Why this matters

This is a conceptual case study — the feature doesn't exist yet in Google Maps. But the opportunity is real, and the design principles translate directly to any product that serves users across different contexts.
