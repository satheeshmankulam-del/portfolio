The Channels
I Didn't Ask For

WhatsApp launched Channels — a broadcast feature — and added it to every user's app without asking. This is a study of how forced feature introduction, unsolicited notifications, and aggressive discoverability erode trust in a product people use for deeply personal communication.

01 — Observation

When a product adds
what you didn't want

WhatsApp Channels appeared in my app one day with no opt-in, a prominent new tab, and auto-suggested channels I'd never heard of pre-followed in my feed. Within 24 hours, I had notifications from organisations I'd never chosen to follow.

The feature itself isn't inherently bad — a broadcast/follow system has genuine utility. But the implementation assumed consent that was never given, and placed a fundamentally different use case (following public channels) inside an app built entirely on the trust of private messaging.

WhatsApp's entire brand equity is built on privacy. Adding a public feed without opt-in isn't just a UX mistake — it's a brand contradiction.

02 — Analysis

Four UX violations
in one feature launch

🚨
Zero opt-in
Channels was enabled by default for all users. There was no choice, no explanation, no chance to decline before the feature was live in your app.
🔔
Unsolicited notifications
Auto-followed channels sent notifications without permission. WhatsApp had a pre-existing, well-understood notification permission model — this bypassed it.
📍
Prime real-estate hijack
The Channels tab was placed at the same level as Chats, Calls, and Status — the four core pillars of the app. A new, unchosen feature was given equal weight to private messaging.
🔀
Context collapse
Mixing public broadcast content with private messaging in the same app and notification stream collapses two very different mental contexts into one uncomfortable space.
03 — The Fix

How feature launches
should treat existing users

The principle is additive, not insertive. New features should extend an existing user's experience, not interrupt it. For an app with 2 billion users who have calibrated expectations about what the app does, any change to the fundamental structure deserves an opt-in flow.

A respectful Channels launch would have: explained the feature, offered a clear opt-in, defaulted to no followed channels, placed the tab below the fold until chosen, and never sent a notification from something the user hadn't deliberately followed.

04 — Takeaway

The cost of
assuming consent

Trust, once broken, is expensive to rebuild. WhatsApp's strength is that people use it for things that matter — family group chats, sensitive conversations, important coordination. Adding an unsolicited public feed into that context isn't just friction. It's a violation of the implicit contract.

Product teams at scale need a different standard for change management than startups. When your product is woven into billions of people's daily lives, 'move fast' is not a responsible default.
